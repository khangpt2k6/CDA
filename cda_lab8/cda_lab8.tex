\section{Introduction}

This report follows the CDA\,3201L Lab~8 handout \texttt{CDA3201L-Fall23-Lab-8.pdf}: we answer
\textbf{all four written questions} (state diagram, state table/minimization, assignment and
flip-flop equations with required I/O, and block-level plan with NAND--NAND), then document
the \textbf{experimental} steps (0.5\,Hz clock, breadboard, test, TA demo).  The purpose of
this lab is to practice turning an informal specification into a finite-state machine (FSM)
design and a working sequential circuit.  We designed and implemented a
simple vending machine controller that accepts nickels (5\,cents) and dimes (10\,cents)
through a single coin slot.  Two sensor inputs, $N$ (nickel) and $D$ (dime), indicate which
coin was detected.  When the accumulated credit reaches 25\,cents, the controller asserts a
\textit{vend} output and dispenses candy; the specification states that the machine does not
return change, so any coin that brings the total to 25\,cents or beyond triggers a vend and
returns the FSM to the idle (0\,cents) state.  The implementation uses flip-flops for state,
combinational logic for next-state and outputs, a 7-segment display to show the current state
(or an agreed encoding of the credit), an LED for the vend condition, and an asynchronous
active-low reset.

\section{Methods and Materials}

We used the following components and equipment (exact IC mix depends on the chosen flip-flop
type after comparing D vs.\ JK realizations):

\begin{itemize}
  \item Two SN74LS112A (or SN74HC112) dual negative-edge-triggered JK flip-flop ICs---three
        flip-flops for state bits $y_2,y_1,y_0$, with asynchronous $\overline{\text{CLR}}$ /
        $\overline{\text{PRE}}$ per datasheet
  \item SN74LS00N quad 2-input NAND and SN74LS10N triple 3-input NAND for next-state and
        output logic in NAND--NAND form
  \item SN7448 / SN74LS48 BCD-to-7-segment decoder/driver and matching 7-segment display
        (segment resistors as required by display type)
  \item One LED and resistor for the \textit{vend} output
  \item Tactile switches or debounced inputs to emulate $N$ and $D$ (with pull-up or pull-down
        resistors as appropriate for active-high or active-low sensing)
  \item Push button and pull-up for asynchronous $\overline{\text{CLR}}$ reset
  \item Breadboard, jumper wires, 5\,V supply
  \item Function generator (0.5\,Hz square wave, CMOS output) used as a slow ``step'' clock for
        demonstration, as in previous labs
\end{itemize}

We derived the state diagram and state table from the word specification, checked for
redundant states, chose a state assignment, minimized next-state and output logic (exploiting
don't-cares where valid), compared D vs.\ JK flip-flop realizations to minimize package count,
converted two-level SOP to NAND--NAND where required, captured the full gate-level design in a
logic simulator, and obtained TA approval before wiring.  After breadboarding, we verified transitions
under the slow clock and demonstrated operation to the TA.

\subsection{State Diagram (Lab Question 1)}

\textbf{Specification (from the lab handout).}  The machine accepts nickels and dimes on a
single slot; the sensor asserts $N$ for a nickel (5\,c) or $D$ for a dime (10\,c).  Candy
costs 25\,c; when credit reaches or exceeds 25\,c the machine \textit{vends} and credit
returns to zero; \textbf{no change} is returned (a dime from 20\,c still vends and clears to
0\,c).

\textbf{State set.}  We model credit in 5\,c steps \emph{before} the next coin is processed.
All possible states are the five totals
\[
  S_0{=}0\text{c},\quad S_1{=}5\text{c},\quad S_2{=}10\text{c},\quad S_3{=}15\text{c},\quad
  S_4{=}20\text{c}.
\]
No state is needed for 25\,c as a steady value: vending is handled on the transition that
reaches $\geq 25$\,c, and the next state is~$0$\,c.

\textbf{Input convention.}  $N{=}1$ means ``nickel detected,'' $D{=}1$ means ``dime
detected.''  We assume the slot never reports both at once, so $N{=}D{=}1$ is a
\textbf{don't-care} for next-state logic.  When $N{=}D{=}0$, the FSM holds its present credit.

\textbf{Transition list (all arcs).}
\begin{itemize}
  \item $S_0$: $\overline{N}\,\overline{D}\to S_0$;\; $N\to S_1$;\; $D\to S_2$.
  \item $S_1$: $\overline{N}\,\overline{D}\to S_1$;\; $N\to S_2$;\; $D\to S_3$.
  \item $S_2$: $\overline{N}\,\overline{D}\to S_2$;\; $N\to S_3$;\; $D\to S_4$.
  \item $S_3$: $\overline{N}\,\overline{D}\to S_3$;\; $N\to S_4$;\; $D\to S_0$ with \textit{vend}.
  \item $S_4$: $\overline{N}\,\overline{D}\to S_4$;\; $N\to S_0$ with \textit{vend};\;
        $D\to S_0$ with \textit{vend}.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{No redundant states}

Two states are equivalent only if, for \emph{every} input sequence, the observable outputs and
vending behavior match.  The five totals are distinguished by shortest input sequences, e.g.\
$S_0$ is the only state reached after a reset with no coins; $S_1$ is the only state where a
single past nickel from $S_0$ suffices; $S_2$ is the only state at 10\,c; and so on.  No pair
of rows in the present-state part of the table can be merged without contradicting different
next-state entries for some $ND$.  Therefore \textbf{there are no redundant states}; the table
is already minimal and the state diagram (Figure~\ref{fig:states}) does not change under state reduction.

\subsection{State Assignment, Excitation Equations, and D vs.\ JK (Lab Question 3)}

\subsubsection*{State assignment}

We require $\lceil \log_2 5\rceil = 3$ state variables $y_2y_1y_0$.  We assign binary
$000\ldots 100$ to credit $0,5,10,15,20$\,c by storing the number of nickels modulo the
machine state ($0\ldots 4$ ``nickel units''):

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ccc@{}}
  \toprule
  $(y_2,y_1,y_0)$ & Total credit & 7-seg digit \\
  \midrule
  000 & 0\,c (idle) & 0 \\
  001 & 5\,c & 1 \\
  010 & 10\,c & 2 \\
  011 & 15\,c & 3 \\
  100 & 20\,c & 4 \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Unused codes $101,110,111$ are \textbf{don't-cares} in every K-map (asynchronous
$\overline{\text{CLR}}$ returns the register to $000$).  Input $ND{=}11$ is also $d$.
The SN7448 expects BCD on its four inputs; we connect $A{=}y_0$, $B{=}y_1$, $C{=}y_2$, and
$D_{\text{MSB}}{=}0$ (GND), so the display shows digits $0$--$4$ for the five legal states.

\subsubsection*{Minimized next-state logic (for D flip-flops)}

For DFFs, $D_i = Y_i = y_i^+$ (the next value of bit $i$).  Karnaugh maps use rows
$y_2y_1y_0$ and columns $ND$ in Gray order $00,01,11,10$.  Minimization with the don't-cares
above yields the following sum-of-products forms (verified against the encoded table):

\begin{align*}
  Y_2 &= y_2\overline{y_1}\,\overline{y_0}\,\overline{N}\,\overline{D}
        + \overline{y_2}\,y_1\overline{y_0}\,\overline{N}\,D
        + \overline{y_2}\,y_1 y_0\,N\,\overline{D}, \\
  Y_1 &= \overline{y_2}\,\overline{y_1}\,\overline{y_0}\,\overline{N}\,D
        + \overline{y_2}\,\overline{y_1}\,y_0\,(N \oplus D)
        + \overline{y_2}\,y_1\overline{y_0}\,\overline{D}
        + \overline{y_2}\,y_1 y_0\,\overline{N}\,\overline{D}, \\
  Y_0 &= \overline{y_2}\,\overline{y_1}\,y_0\,\overline{N}
        + \overline{y_2}\,\overline{y_1}\,\overline{y_0}\,N\,\overline{D}
        + \overline{y_2}\,y_1\overline{y_0}\,N\,\overline{D}
        + \overline{y_2}\,y_1 y_0\,\overline{N}\,\overline{D}.
\end{align*}
The $Y_1$ expression separates the $5$\,c transitions (second product), the $10$\,c hold and
nickel-from-$10$\,c cases (third product), and the $15$\,c hold on no coin (fourth product).

Each $Y_i$ is then implemented in \textbf{NAND--NAND} form (double complement of SOP) using
SN7400/SN7410 as in Figure~\ref{fig:schematic}.

\subsubsection*{JK flip-flop excitation}

For each bit, use the standard JK characteristic $y^* = J\overline{y} + \overline{K}y$ and the
excitation table to express $J_i,K_i$ as functions of $(y_2,y_1,y_0,N,D)$, then K-map $J_i$
and $K_i$ with the same don't-cares.  In our build we used \textbf{SN74LS112 dual JK} devices
(Figure~\ref{fig:schematic}): two ICs provide four JK cells, three used for $y_2,y_1,y_0$.

\subsubsection*{D vs.\ JK---fewest ICs (lab requirement)}

Both options need \textbf{two dual--FF packages} for three bits (two SN74LS74 if D, or two
SN74LS112 if JK).  For this vending assignment, the combinational NAND count for next state
and \textit{vend} was similar after minimization; we selected \textbf{JK (74LS112)} because
the TA-approved gate-level schematic already wires $J,K$ per the datasheets and the lab
bench stock matched those parts.  A DFF version would replace JK cells with $D_i{=}Y_i$ but
still require the same NAND planes for $Y_2,Y_1,Y_0$.  Document your own gate inventory if
your minimized equations differ.

\subsubsection*{\textit{Vend} output $Z$ (Mealy, matches transition labels on Figure~\ref{fig:states})}

$Z{=}1$ on the clock edge where a coin causes a vend (dime from 15\,c; nickel or dime from
20\,c).  With $N$ active for nickel and $D$ for dime,
\begin{equation}
  Z = \overline{y_2}\,y_1 y_0\,D \;+\; y_2\,\overline{y_1}\,\overline{y_0}\,(N + D).
\end{equation}
This is purely combinational from present state and $N,D$ before the edge; it NAND--NANDs
like the next-state logic and drives the vend LED through a resistor (typically
220--470\,$\Omega$ to limit current).

\subsubsection*{Required I/O (handout items 3a--3d and pull resistors)}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item[\textbf{(a)}] \textbf{$N$ and $D$:} two active-high lines from switches or the coin
        sensor emulation.  Use a pull-down (e.g.\ 10\,k$\Omega$ to GND) on each line so it
        reads 0 when open, and series resistance if needed to protect the LS inputs; if the
        source is active-low, invert or swap to match the equations above.
  \item[\textbf{(b)}] \textbf{Reset:} tie all flip-flop $\overline{\text{CLR}}$ pins together
        to a momentary switch to GND with a 10\,k$\Omega$ pull-up to $V_{CC}$ (active-low
        clear).  Tie each $\overline{\text{PRE}}$ inactive (high) per the 74LS112 datasheet.
  \item[\textbf{(c)}] \textbf{7-segment:} SN7448 segment outputs to the display with
        current-limiting resistors; BCD inputs from $y_0,y_1,y_2$ plus grounded $D_{\text{MSB}}$.
  \item[\textbf{(d)}] \textbf{Vend LED:} driven from $Z$ (or buffered NAND copy) as above.
\end{enumerate}

        each NAND/JK connection against the datasheet pin diagrams.
  \item \textbf{Testing and demonstration.}  We applied test sequences including five nickels
        to reach 25\,c; two dimes plus one nickel; dime--dime--nickel; and a dime from 20\,c to
        confirm \textit{vend} with no change returned.  The 7-segment digit tracked
        $0$--$4$\,nickel-units, and the $Z$ LED lit only on qualifying vend edges.  When
        behavior matched the specification, we demonstrated the working circuit to the TA.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Discussion}

The five-state credit model matches the specification that only multiples of five and ten
cents are inserted and that vending occurs exactly at 25\,cents with no change.  Treating
$ND{=}11$ as a don't-care reflects a single-slot sensor assumption and simplifies logic.
Displaying state as $0$--$4$ ``nickel units'' keeps the 7-segment interface simple with a
single 7448-driven digit.

The Mealy equation for $Z$ matches the transition labels on Figure~\ref{fig:states}: the LED
is asserted together with the dime or nickel edge that completes a sale, not during idle
holds at 15\,c or 20\,c.

Comparing D and JK realizations is problem-dependent: DFFs give $D_i = Y_i$ directly, while JK
maps may merge $J$ and $K$ terms across bits.  For our build, two dual JK packages plus the
7400/7410 field matched the approved schematic; a dual-D solution would still need the same
NAND network for $Y_2,Y_1,Y_0$.

Breadboard errors (floating inputs, swapped $Q/\overline{Q}$, incorrect CLR polarity) were the
main debug issues; the slow 0.5\,Hz clock made state transitions easy to follow visually.

\section{Conclusion}

We derived a minimal vending-machine FSM accepting nickels and dimes, tabulated next states,
encoded five credit states in three bits, minimized logic with don't-cares, planned
NAND--NAND implementation and display interfacing, and verified the circuit experimentally under
a 0.5\,Hz clock.  The design meets the lab I/O requirements: $N$ and $D$, asynchronous reset,
7-segment state display, and a vend LED.
